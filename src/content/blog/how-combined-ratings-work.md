# How VA Combined Ratings Work (And Why 70 + 30 ≠ 100%)

One of the most confusing aspects of VA disability compensation is the combined ratings formula. Most veterans assume their percentages are simply added together: 50% + 30% = 80%. But VA math does not work that way, and understanding the difference can change how you prioritize your claims.

## The Combined Ratings Table

The VA uses a **whole person** model. The idea is that you start as a 100% healthy person, and each disability takes away from what is left of your healthy "whole person."

### The Formula Step by Step

1. Take your highest rating first and apply it to 100%.
   - 50% disability → 50 points of disability from 100
   - Remaining whole person: **50%**

2. Apply the next rating to the remaining percentage.
   - 30% of the remaining 50% = 15 additional disability points
   - Running total: 50 + 15 = **65% combined**

3. Apply the next rating to what is now remaining.
   - After 65% combined, the remaining whole person is 35%
   - 10% of 35% = 3.5 additional points
   - Running total: 65 + 3.5 = **68.5% combined**

4. Round to the nearest 10% (5% and above rounds up).
   - 68.5% → **70% final combined rating**

Note that this example uses 50% + 30% + 10%, yet the combined result is only 70% — not 90%.

## Why You Can Never Mathematically Reach 100%

This is a quirk of the formula: because each additional rating is applied to a smaller and smaller remaining percentage, you can stack an infinite number of ratings and never mathematically reach 100%. You can get very close (97%, 98%), but the formula prevents reaching 100% through addition alone.

**The only ways to receive a 100% rating are:**
1. A single disability rated at 100% by the VASRD (e.g., active cancer, certain severe mental health conditions)
2. **TDIU** (Total Disability based on Individual Unemployability) — rated at 100% if your service-connected conditions prevent substantially gainful employment
3. Special Monthly Compensation (SMC) — which can exceed the 100% rate for specific severe conditions

## Rounding Rules

The VA rounds the final combined rating to the nearest 10%:

| Calculated Total | Rounds To |
|---|---|
| 1%–4% | 0% |
| 5%–14% | 10% |
| 15%–24% | 20% |
| 25%–34% | 30% |
| 35%–44% | 40% |
| 45%–54% | 50% |
| 55%–64% | 60% |
| 65%–74% | 70% |
| 75%–84% | 80% |
| 85%–94% | 90% |
| 95%–100% | 100% |

This means a calculated result of 65% rounds up to **70%** — a significant difference in monthly compensation. The rounding effect is one reason why certain combinations (like 60% + 20%) are more advantageous than others.

## The Order of Ratings Matters

Because each rating is applied to the remaining whole person, the VA processes ratings **from highest to lowest**. The order does not change the final result — the mathematical outcome is the same regardless of which rating you process first — but it is important to understand that the VA sequences them by size.

## The Bilateral Factor

If you have service-connected disabilities affecting both sides of the body (both knees, both ears, both arms, etc.), a **10% bilateral factor** is added to the combined value of the bilateral ratings before they are combined with your other ratings.

**Example:**
- Right knee: 20%
- Left knee: 10%
- Combined bilateral: 28% → +10% bilateral factor = 28 + 2.8 = **30.8% → 31%** from bilateral disabilities
- This 31% is then combined with your other ratings in the normal formula

The bilateral factor rewards veterans with symmetric disabilities and can meaningfully increase the final combined rating.

## Practical Implications for Your Claims

### Diminishing Returns at Higher Ratings

At a combined rating of 90%, the remaining whole person is only 10%. A new 50% rating adds only 5 additional disability points (50% of 10%), moving you from 90% to 95% combined — which rounds to 90% (unchanged). At high combined ratings, individual condition ratings have less impact on the final number.

This is why TDIU is often more valuable than chasing the last few percentage points.

### The Strategic Value of New Secondary Claims

Despite diminishing returns at high combined ratings, additional claims still matter:

1. **Ratings below 70% combined** benefit significantly from each new rating. Moving from 60% to 70% is an increase of approximately $380/month (2026 rates).
2. **Qualifying for TDIU** — if you are at 60% from a single disability or 70% combined with one condition at 40%+, even a small additional rating may not matter as much as demonstrating unemployability.
3. **Special Monthly Compensation (SMC)** — certain conditions qualify you for SMC regardless of your combined rating, often exceeding the 100% compensation level.

## Example: Real-World Combinations

| Ratings | VA Combined | Rounds To |
|---|---|---|
| 70% + 30% | 79% | 80% |
| 70% + 50% | 85% | 90% |
| 60% + 40% | 76% | 80% |
| 50% + 30% + 20% | 72% | 70% |
| 50% + 30% + 10% | 68.5% | 70% |
| 40% + 20% + 10% | 56.8% | 60% |

Notice that 70% + 30% = **80%**, not 100%. And 60% + 40% = **80%** — same result despite different inputs.

## Why This Matters for Filing Strategy

1. **File all conditions** — even low-percentage ratings (10%, 20%) add meaningful value at lower combined percentages and can push you over a rounding threshold.
2. **Identify secondary conditions** — conditions caused or worsened by your primary service-connected disabilities are ratable and often easier to win than primary claims.
3. **Target rounding thresholds** — if your calculated combined rating is 65%, 75%, or 85%, even a single 10% rating pushes you to the next tier.
4. **Consider TDIU** if your combined rating is 60%+ (single) or 70%+ (multiple) and you cannot maintain substantially gainful employment.
