# Lumbar Strain VA Disability Rating Guide

Lower back conditions are among the most frequently claimed musculoskeletal disabilities for veterans. Whether it resulted from carrying heavy rucksacks, airborne operations, vehicle-borne IED blasts, or years of physical labor, a lumbar spine condition can significantly limit a veteran's quality of life after service.

## How the VA Rates Lumbar Spine Conditions

The thoracolumbar spine (mid and lower back) is rated under 38 CFR 4.71a using the General Rating Formula for Diseases and Injuries of the Spine, primarily based on **range of motion** measurements:

**10% — Forward flexion greater than 60 degrees but not greater than 85 degrees**
Or combined range of motion greater than 120 degrees but not greater than 235 degrees. Also assigned for muscle spasm, guarding, or localized tenderness not resulting in abnormal gait or spinal contour.

**20% — Forward flexion greater than 30 degrees but not greater than 60 degrees**
Or combined range of motion not greater than 120 degrees. Also assigned when muscle spasm or guarding is severe enough to result in abnormal gait or abnormal spinal contour (scoliosis, reversed lordosis, or abnormal kyphosis).

**40% — Forward flexion 30 degrees or less, or favorable ankylosis**
This rating represents a significant limitation where the veteran cannot bend forward more than 30 degrees.

**50% — Unfavorable ankylosis of the entire thoracolumbar spine**
The spine is fixed in a position that is not neutral.

**100% — Unfavorable ankylosis of the entire spine**
Both the cervical and thoracolumbar spine are fixed.

### Intervertebral Disc Syndrome (IVDS)

If your condition involves disc herniation, bulging discs, or degenerative disc disease with incapacitating episodes (periods requiring bed rest prescribed by a physician), the VA can rate under the IVDS formula if it produces a higher rating:

- **10%** — Incapacitating episodes totaling at least 1 week but less than 2 weeks during the past 12 months
- **20%** — At least 2 weeks but less than 4 weeks
- **40%** — At least 4 weeks but less than 6 weeks
- **60%** — At least 6 weeks

## Key Evidence for Your Claim

- **Service treatment records** documenting back complaints, injuries, or treatment during service
- **Current imaging** (X-ray, MRI) showing degenerative changes, disc herniation, or other structural pathology
- **Range of motion measurements** from a medical provider — these directly determine your rating level
- **Documentation of flare-ups** and how they limit your function. The VA must consider pain on motion and functional loss during flare-ups per *DeLuca v. Brown* and *Mitchell v. Shinseki*

## C&P Exam Tips

- **Do not push through pain during range of motion testing.** Stop at the point where pain limits your motion. The examiner must record where pain begins.
- **Report your worst days** when describing functional limitations. The exam captures a single snapshot; make sure the examiner understands how your back affects you over time.
- **Request Duplex testing** or at minimum ensure the examiner measures range of motion twice, as required by *Correia v. McDonald* (active, passive, weight-bearing, and non-weight-bearing).
- **Describe radiculopathy** if you have pain, numbness, or tingling shooting into your legs. This can be rated separately under the peripheral nerve codes and add significantly to your combined rating.

## Secondary Conditions

Back conditions frequently cause or worsen:

- **Radiculopathy** (shooting pain down legs) — rated separately at 10%-60% per extremity
- **Knee conditions** from altered gait mechanics
- **Hip conditions** from compensatory movement patterns
- **Depression or anxiety** from chronic pain and limited mobility

Each of these secondary claims can be filed individually and will add to your combined disability rating.
