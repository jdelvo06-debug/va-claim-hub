# Fibromyalgia VA Disability Rating Guide

Fibromyalgia is a chronic pain disorder characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and cognitive difficulties ("fibro fog"). It affects a significant number of veterans, particularly those with histories of physical trauma, PTSD, or other high-stress service experiences.

## How the VA Rates Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is rated under 38 CFR 4.71a, **Diagnostic Code 5025**. There are three possible ratings:

**10% — Widespread musculoskeletal pain and tender points**
Symptoms are present but controlled by continuous medication, or episodic with periods of remission.

**20% — Constant or nearly constant pain**
Widespread pain and tender points that are present constantly or nearly constantly, requiring medication for control. This is the most common rating assigned for fibromyalgia that significantly affects daily function.

**40% — Totally incapacitating**
Episodic attacks that are so severe and frequent that the veteran is prevented from working or performing activities of daily living. This rating requires documentation of episodes that render the veteran totally incapacitated.

> **Note:** Unlike many other conditions, fibromyalgia has a maximum VA rating of 40%. However, secondary conditions caused by fibromyalgia (such as depression, sleep apnea, or cognitive impairment) can be rated separately and added to your overall combined rating.

## Establishing Service Connection

### Direct Service Connection

Direct service connection requires showing that fibromyalgia began during military service or was caused by an in-service event. Evidence includes:

- In-service treatment records documenting widespread pain, fatigue, or multiple tender point complaints
- Documentation of in-service physical trauma (accidents, injuries, or prolonged physical hardship)
- History of physical stressors unique to military service (extreme sleep deprivation, high-impact physical training, prolonged exposure to extreme temperatures)

### Secondary Service Connection

Fibromyalgia is frequently claimed secondary to other service-connected conditions:

- **PTSD and other mental health conditions** — Research strongly supports a bidirectional relationship between trauma and fibromyalgia. Chronic stress and hyperactivation of the nervous system are thought to be central to fibromyalgia's pathophysiology.
- **Physical injuries** — Chronic pain from service-connected orthopedic conditions (knee, back, shoulder) can sensitize the central nervous system and trigger fibromyalgia.
- **Gulf War Syndrome** — Veterans of the Gulf War have a significantly elevated rate of fibromyalgia and may be eligible for presumptive service connection.

### Gulf War Presumption

Veterans who served in the Southwest Asia theater of operations (including the Gulf War, Operation Iraqi Freedom, and Operation Enduring Freedom) may be eligible for **presumptive service connection** for fibromyalgia under 38 CFR 3.317 as an "undiagnosed illness" or "medically unexplained chronic multisymptom illness." This presumption applies if:

- The veteran served in the Southwest Asia theater
- The fibromyalgia manifested to a compensable degree (10% or more) during active duty or any time thereafter
- There is no clear military or combat-related cause that would disqualify the presumption

This is one of the most important and underused tools for Gulf War veterans with fibromyalgia.

## Key Evidence for Your Claim

- **Rheumatology records** confirming the diagnosis using current ACR (American College of Rheumatology) criteria — specifically the widespread pain index and symptom severity scale
- **Treatment records** documenting the frequency, severity, and functional impact of pain
- **Medication history** — particularly use of Lyrica, Cymbalta, Savella, or other fibromyalgia-specific medications
- **Functional assessments** describing impact on daily activities, work capacity, and mobility
- **Nexus letter** from a rheumatologist or physician explaining the connection between your military service and current fibromyalgia diagnosis

## C&P Exam Tips

- **Describe your pain on your worst days**, not average or good days
- **List all tender points** and how many are active on a typical bad day versus a good day
- **Document your fatigue and cognitive symptoms** — fibro fog (difficulty concentrating, poor short-term memory, word-finding problems) is a ratable symptom
- **Describe functional limitations**: Can you sit at a desk for 8 hours? Stand at a register? Lift? Drive? These daily function questions are directly relevant to your rating.
- **Track symptoms in the weeks before your exam** — a written symptom log is powerful supporting evidence

## Secondary Claims Linked to Fibromyalgia

Veterans with fibromyalgia should evaluate:

- **[Depression](/conditions/depression)** — chronic pain and fibromyalgia are strongly associated with major depressive disorder
- **[Sleep apnea](/conditions/sleep-apnea)** — chronic pain and sleep disruption in fibromyalgia often co-occur with sleep-disordered breathing
- **[GERD](/conditions/gerd)** — gastrointestinal symptoms are common fibromyalgia comorbidities
- **IBS (irritable bowel syndrome)** — frequently co-occurs with fibromyalgia and is separately ratable

## Common Mistakes

1. **Filing without a formal rheumatology diagnosis** — fibromyalgia must be diagnosed by a qualified physician using accepted clinical criteria
2. **Not pursuing the Gulf War presumption** — many eligible veterans are unaware of this pathway
3. **Ignoring secondary conditions** — the 40% cap on fibromyalgia makes secondary condition claims especially important for maximizing your overall combined rating
4. **Downplaying fluctuating symptoms** — fibromyalgia flares and remits; document your worst patterns, not just your current state on the day of the exam
