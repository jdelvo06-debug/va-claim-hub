# Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) VA Disability Rating Guide

Traumatic brain injury is one of the signature wounds of post-9/11 combat, caused by blast exposure, vehicle accidents, falls, and direct head trauma. TBI ranges from mild (concussion) to severe (penetrating brain injury), and its long-term effects can impact cognition, memory, mood, vision, balance, and quality of life for decades.

## How the VA Rates TBI

TBI is rated under 38 CFR 4.124a, Diagnostic Code 8045. Rather than a single overall rating, the VA evaluates **ten separate facets** of TBI residuals and assigns the highest applicable rating from those facets:

### The Ten Facets of TBI Rating

1. **Memory, attention, concentration, executive functions**
2. **Judgment**
3. **Social interaction**
4. **Orientation**
5. **Motor activity**
6. **Visual-spatial orientation**
7. **Subjective symptoms** (headaches, dizziness, fatigue, etc.)
8. **Neurobehavioral effects** (irritability, impulsivity, mood changes)
9. **Communication**
10. **Consciousness**

Each facet is rated on a scale from 0 to 3 (mild, moderate, moderately severe, severe), and the overall TBI rating is based on the **highest single facet** score, not an average.

### Rating Levels

**10%** — Mild residuals (one or more facets rated "mild")

**40%** — Moderate residuals (one or more facets rated "moderate")

**70%** — Moderately severe residuals (one or more facets rated "moderately severe")

**100%** — Severe residuals or total occupational and social impairment

## Key Evidence for Your TBI Claim

### Service Records Documenting the Injury
- Line-of-duty documentation of the incident
- Military Occupational Specialty exposure to blast events (MOS codes in EOD, infantry, etc.)
- Field medical records or combat casualty care documentation
- MHAT (Mental Health Advisory Team) records or post-deployment health assessments
- Documentation of loss of consciousness, altered consciousness, or post-traumatic amnesia

### Current Medical Evidence
- Neuropsychological testing evaluating memory, processing speed, executive function, and attention
- Neuroimaging (CT, MRI) — note that many mild TBI cases show no structural abnormality on imaging, which does not mean no injury occurred
- A DBQ (Disability Benefits Questionnaire) completed by a qualified examiner covering all ten facets

### Nexus Letter
A nexus letter from a neurologist or neuropsychologist linking your current TBI residuals to the in-service incident is essential, especially for claims filed years after separation.

## Secondary Conditions to TBI

TBI frequently causes or worsens several ratable conditions:

- **Depression and anxiety** — one of the most common TBI comorbidities
- **PTSD** — TBI and PTSD frequently co-occur in combat veterans; the VA must rate both
- **Sleep disturbances** — insomnia and sleep apnea are common TBI residuals
- **Migraines** — post-traumatic headache is among the most prevalent TBI symptom
- **Tinnitus** — blast-related TBI often causes concurrent auditory nerve damage
- **Seizure disorders** — particularly after moderate to severe TBI

## C&P Exam Tips

TBI C&P exams are conducted by a VA neurologist or neuropsychologist and may involve formal cognitive testing:

- **Bring a detailed written history** of the incident, symptoms at the time, and how symptoms have changed since
- **Document cognitive symptoms concretely**: describe specific situations where memory, concentration, or executive function failures have caused problems at work or home
- **Don't perform better than you function day-to-day** — if you have good days and bad days, describe the bad days
- **Bring a support person** who can corroborate behavioral and cognitive changes they have observed
- **List all current medications**, including any prescribed for headaches, sleep, mood, or seizures

## Common Mistakes

1. **Filing only for TBI without filing secondary claims** — Depression, PTSD, sleep apnea, and migraines secondary to TBI can significantly increase combined rating
2. **Not requesting a neuropsychological evaluation** — Cognitive residuals are often the most disabling and are easily missed without formal testing
3. **Relying only on imaging** — Many veterans wrongly believe that a "normal MRI" means they cannot claim TBI. Mild TBI rarely shows on standard imaging.
4. **Missing the exposure documentation** — Service members assigned to units with frequent blast exposure have a strong basis for presumptive TBI even without specific incident records
