# Evidence Types That Win VA Disability Claims

The difference between an approved claim and a denied claim usually comes down to evidence. The VA decides claims based on a **preponderance of evidence** — if the evidence for service connection is at least as strong as the evidence against it (the "at least as likely as not" standard), the benefit of the doubt goes to the veteran. Knowing which types of evidence carry the most weight lets you build a winning claim file.

## The Three Elements of Every Claim

To win a VA disability claim, you must establish three things:

1. **Current diagnosis** — A current, documented medical diagnosis of the condition you are claiming
2. **In-service event or incurrence** — Evidence that something happened during your military service that caused or contributed to the condition
3. **Nexus** — A medical link connecting #1 and #2

Evidence that addresses all three elements simultaneously is the strongest possible claim submission.

## Tier 1: Highest-Weight Evidence

### Private Nexus Letter

A **nexus letter** from a qualified medical professional is the single most impactful piece of evidence in most claims. A well-written nexus letter:

- Identifies the veteran by name and diagnoses the condition
- References the veteran's service history and any in-service events
- Uses the required legal language: *"at least as likely as not"* that the condition is related to service
- Cites peer-reviewed medical literature supporting the connection
- Is signed by a physician, psychologist, or appropriate specialist (not a chiropractor or nurse practitioner for most complex claims)

**Why it wins claims:** The C&P examiner's opinion is the VA's own evidence. A contradicting private nexus opinion creates a conflict that the VA must resolve in your favor under the benefit-of-the-doubt standard — as long as your opinion is at least as credible as the examiner's.

### Completed DBQ from a Private Physician

A **Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ)** completed by your treating physician captures the exact clinical findings the VA uses to rate your condition. Unlike a general medical letter, a DBQ is formatted to answer the specific questions raters use.

**Why it wins claims:** A VA C&P examiner may spend 20–30 minutes with you. Your treating physician who has seen you for years can document functional limitations far more accurately. A thoroughly completed DBQ — especially for musculoskeletal conditions where range-of-motion measurements directly determine rating percentages — can significantly outperform a VA exam.

### Service Treatment Records (STRs)

Your **service treatment records** are the documentary record of your medical care during service. An in-service documentation of your condition — even a single sick call record noting the symptom — establishes the in-service incurrence element without requiring a nexus letter.

**What to look for:**
- Direct documentation of the claimed condition
- Documentation of symptoms even if the diagnosis was different (e.g., "low back pain" entries for a lumbar strain claim)
- Injury reports, line of duty investigations, or accident reports
- Mental health referrals or behavioral health notes

**How to obtain them:** Request your STRs through the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) via a Standard Form 180 (SF-180), or through the VA's VBMS if you are working with a VSO.

## Tier 2: Strong Supporting Evidence

### Medical Treatment Records (Post-Service)

VA treatment records and private medical records documenting your condition after service show the **continuity of the condition** and its current severity. The VA should automatically pull your VA treatment records — but you must submit private records yourself.

**Key elements to highlight:**
- Diagnosis dates (earlier diagnoses support earlier effective dates)
- Severity documentation and functional limitations
- Prescribed medications and treatments
- Specialist referrals confirming the diagnosis

### Lay Statements (Buddy Statements)

**38 CFR 3.303** explicitly recognizes lay testimony as competent evidence. For conditions that are observable without medical training (pain, behavioral changes, visible conditions), lay statements from veterans, family members, and fellow service members carry real weight.

**Effective buddy statement content:**
- The witness's relationship to the veteran and when/how they observed the condition
- Specific, dated observations ("In January 2005, when John returned from deployment, he could not sleep more than two hours without nightmares")
- Describe the *change* — what the veteran was like before service vs. after
- Avoid vague, conclusory statements ("he was different after the military") in favor of specific, observable details

**The veteran's own statement** (submitted on VA Form 21-4142 or as a written personal statement) is also competent lay evidence. Describe the in-service event in detail: dates, locations, unit, what happened. For PTSD and MST claims especially, a detailed personal statement of the stressor is essential.

### Military Personnel Records

Your **DD-214** and military personnel records establish your service dates, MOS, deployment history, and awards that help corroborate your claimed in-service events.

- Combat decorations (Purple Heart, Combat Action Badge, Combat Infantryman Badge) corroborate combat exposure for PTSD claims
- Deployment records to Agent Orange exposure zones support presumptive claims
- MOS documentation helps establish occupational noise exposure for hearing loss claims
- Performance evaluations showing a decline over time can support mental health claims

## Tier 3: Useful Supporting Evidence

### Independent Medical Examinations (IMEs)

A formal **Independent Medical Examination** from a board-certified specialist — distinct from a treating physician's letter — is the gold standard for contested or complex claims. An IME physician reviews your full medical history, examines you, and produces a detailed written opinion with citations to medical literature.

IMEs are expensive ($800–$2,500+) but are often worth the investment for higher-value claims (70%+ ratings, TDIU, or SMC claims).

### Research and Medical Literature

Peer-reviewed studies, CDC data, and medical textbook excerpts do not win claims on their own — but they support and strengthen a nexus letter. A nexus physician who cites specific studies establishing the link between, for example, sleep apnea and PTSD, is more persuasive than one who states only a clinical opinion.

### Social Security Records

If you have been awarded **SSDI**, submit those records to the VA. The VA is not bound by Social Security's determination, but a federal disability finding supports both your diagnosis and the severity of your functional impairment.

## Common Evidence Mistakes

**1. Filing without a nexus letter when service connection is not obvious**
Many veterans file claims citing only their service dates and a diagnosis. Without a medical opinion connecting the diagnosis to service, the VA will deny for lack of nexus. The fix: obtain a private nexus letter before filing.

**2. Submitting a generic medical letter instead of a DBQ**
A letter that says "John has PTSD" is far weaker than a completed mental health DBQ that documents specific symptoms, their frequency and severity, and the resulting occupational and social impairment. Always ask your physician to complete the applicable DBQ.

**3. Not requesting your C-file before appealing**
Your **claims file (C-file)** contains every document the VA used to decide your claim. Reviewing it often reveals evidence the VA missed, C&P exam errors, or rating calculation mistakes. Request it before filing any appeal.

**4. Downplaying symptoms to the C&P examiner**
Veterans are trained to be stoic. This works against you in a C&P exam. Describe your worst days honestly — how your condition affects you at its worst, not on a good day.

## Building a Winning Evidence Package

For most claims, the optimal evidence package is:

1. **Service treatment records** showing in-service incurrence or symptoms
2. **Current diagnosis** from a treating physician or recent evaluation
3. **Private nexus letter** establishing the service connection
4. **Completed DBQ** documenting current severity
5. **Personal statement** describing the in-service event and current impact
6. **Buddy statements** from family or fellow veterans if relevant

This package addresses all three claim elements and gives the VA no basis for denial.
