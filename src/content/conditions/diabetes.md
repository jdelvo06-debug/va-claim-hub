# Diabetes Mellitus Type 2 VA Disability Rating Guide

Type 2 diabetes mellitus is one of the most prevalent Agent Orange presumptive conditions, affecting hundreds of thousands of Vietnam-era veterans. It is also the gateway to some of the most significant secondary condition ratings available, with diabetic complications able to push combined ratings to 70%, 80%, or higher.

## Agent Orange Presumptive Service Connection

Veterans who were exposed to Agent Orange or other herbicide agents do not need to prove a direct link between their diabetes and military service. Under **38 CFR 3.309(e)**, the VA will presume service connection for Type 2 diabetes if the veteran:

1. **Was exposed to herbicide agents** (Agent Orange, Agent Blue, Agent White, etc.) during service, AND
2. **Has a current diagnosis of Type 2 diabetes mellitus**

### Who Qualifies for the Agent Orange Presumption

**Vietnam veterans**: Anyone who served in the Republic of Vietnam between January 9, 1962, and May 7, 1975, is presumed to have been exposed — boots on the ground is the standard; offshore service (Blue Water Navy) veterans who served in the territorial waters also qualify.

**C-123 aircraft veterans**: Air Force reservists who flew or maintained C-123 aircraft used to spray herbicides between 1969 and 1986 qualify.

**Korea DMZ veterans**: Veterans who served on or near the Korean DMZ between September 1, 1967, and August 31, 1971.

**Thailand air base veterans**: Veterans who served at certain Royal Thai Air Force bases where tactical herbicides were stored or used.

**PACT Act expansion (2022)**: The PACT Act expanded Agent Orange benefits to additional locations and conflicts. Confirm your eligibility with a VSO or at VA.gov.

> **No nexus letter required** for presumptive claims. You need only establish your service location and a current Type 2 diabetes diagnosis.

## How the VA Rates Diabetes

Diabetes is rated under 38 CFR 4.119, **Diagnostic Code 7913**:

**10% — Manageable by restricted diet only**
No insulin or oral hypoglycemic agents required. Diet alone controls blood sugar.

**20% — Oral hypoglycemic agent OR insulin and a restricted diet**
Requires medication, with or without dietary restriction, but without complications.

**40% — Insulin, a restricted diet, and regulation of activities**
The veteran requires insulin and must limit activities due to diabetes management or complications. Frequent episodes of hypoglycemia or episodes requiring medical intervention fall here.

**60% — Insulin, restricted diet, regulation of activities, and with episodes of ketoacidosis or hypoglycemic reactions requiring hospitalization more than twice a year, or with progressive loss of weight and strength**

**100% — Requiring more than one daily injection of insulin, plus dietary restrictions, and progressive complications**

### Important Rating Tip

Many veterans with well-controlled diabetes are underrated at 10% or 20% when they could qualify for 40% if their **activities are regulated** by diabetes management. "Regulation of activities" includes things like:
- Avoiding certain jobs, shifts, or physical activities due to hypoglycemia risk
- Needing to eat at specific times, limiting travel, or avoiding irregular schedules
- Dietary management that limits social or occupational activities

## Secondary Conditions — Where the Real Value Is

Diabetes mellitus causes a wide range of complications, many of which are independently ratable as secondary conditions. The cumulative effect of diabetes ratings plus secondary condition ratings frequently results in much higher combined ratings:

### High-Value Secondary Conditions

**Diabetic Peripheral Neuropathy**
Nerve damage causing numbness, tingling, burning, or pain in the hands and feet. Each affected extremity is rated separately:
- Peripheral neuropathy of the upper extremities: DC 8515 (radial), DC 8516 (ulnar), DC 8517 (median)
- Peripheral neuropathy of the lower extremities: DC 8520 (sciatic), DC 8530 (femoral)
- Ratings range from 10% to 80% per extremity depending on severity

**Diabetic Eye Disease (Retinopathy)**
Retinopathy can cause significant vision impairment and is rated based on corrected visual acuity under 38 CFR 4.79.

**Diabetic Kidney Disease (Nephropathy)**
Rated under DC 7531 based on kidney function. Advanced nephropathy requiring dialysis is rated at 100%.

**Erectile Dysfunction**
A common complication of diabetic neuropathy and vascular disease. While rated at 0% as a condition, it entitles veterans to a separate Special Monthly Compensation (SMC-K) payment in addition to their combined rating.

**Cardiovascular Disease**
Diabetes is a major risk factor for coronary artery disease, hypertension, and heart failure, all of which can be claimed secondary to the service-connected diabetes.

**Hypertension**
Hypertension frequently co-occurs with and is worsened by diabetes. See our [hypertension guide](/conditions/hypertension).

**Depression**
Chronic illness and pain from diabetic complications have well-documented associations with depression. See our [depression guide](/conditions/depression).

## Building a Strong Claim

### For Presumptive Claims
1. Confirm your service location qualifies for the Agent Orange presumption
2. Obtain a current Type 2 diabetes diagnosis from your treating physician
3. File VA Form 21-526EZ citing presumptive service connection under 38 CFR 3.309(e)
4. Simultaneously file for secondary conditions — do not wait

### Evidence to Gather
- Service records confirming qualifying service location (DD-214 is usually sufficient)
- Current diagnosis, most recent HbA1c results, and complete medical history
- List of all current diabetes medications (insulin type, dose, frequency)
- Documentation of any activity restrictions imposed by your physician due to diabetes
- Records of any hospitalizations for hypoglycemia, ketoacidosis, or complications

## C&P Exam Tips

- **Bring all medication records** including insulin types and dosing schedule
- **Describe activity restrictions clearly**: What can't you do because of your diabetes that you could do before?
- **Bring documentation of hypoglycemic episodes**: ER visits, glucose logs, or physician notes
- **If claiming secondary neuropathy**, describe the specific symptoms in each affected extremity

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not filing for secondary conditions simultaneously** — Diabetic neuropathy, retinopathy, and nephropathy claims should be filed at the same time as the primary diabetes claim
2. **Accepting a 10% or 20% rating when 40% is justified** — Review whether your activities are actually regulated by your diabetes management
3. **Vietnam Blue Water Navy veterans not filing** — Many are unaware they now qualify after recent legislative changes
4. **Missing the SMC-K for erectile dysfunction** — This is a separate payment entirely independent of disability percentages
