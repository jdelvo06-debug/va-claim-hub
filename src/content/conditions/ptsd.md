# PTSD VA Disability Rating Guide

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder is one of the most commonly claimed conditions among veterans, and for good reason — combat exposure, military sexual trauma (MST), and other in-service stressors can leave lasting psychological effects that persist for decades after separation.

## How the VA Rates PTSD

PTSD is rated under 38 CFR 4.130, Diagnostic Code 9411, using the General Rating Formula for Mental Disorders. The VA assigns ratings at **0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, or 100%** based on the severity of occupational and social impairment.

### Rating Breakdown

**0% — Diagnosed but Minimal Symptoms**
A formal diagnosis exists, but symptoms are not severe enough to interfere with occupational or social functioning, or symptoms are controlled by continuous medication.

**10% — Mild Impairment**
Occupational and social impairment due to mild or transient symptoms that decrease work efficiency only during periods of significant stress, or symptoms controlled by medication.

**30% — Occasional Decrease in Work Efficiency**
Occupational and social impairment with occasional decrease in work efficiency and intermittent periods of inability to perform occupational tasks. Symptoms may include depressed mood, anxiety, suspiciousness, chronic sleep impairment, and mild memory loss.

**50% — Reduced Reliability and Productivity**
Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity due to symptoms such as flattened affect, circumstantial speech, panic attacks more than once a week, difficulty understanding complex commands, impaired short and long-term memory, impaired judgment, disturbances of motivation and mood, and difficulty establishing effective work and social relationships.

**70% — Deficiencies in Most Areas**
Occupational and social impairment with deficiencies in most areas including work, school, family relations, judgment, thinking, and mood. Symptoms may include suicidal ideation, obsessional rituals, illogical speech, near-continuous panic or depression, impaired impulse control, spatial disorientation, neglect of personal appearance, difficulty adapting to stressful circumstances, and inability to establish and maintain effective relationships.

**100% — Total Occupational and Social Impairment**
Total occupational and social impairment due to symptoms such as gross impairment in thought processes, persistent delusions or hallucinations, grossly inappropriate behavior, persistent danger of hurting self or others, intermittent inability to perform activities of daily living, disorientation to time or place, and memory loss for names of close relatives or own name.

## Key Evidence for Your PTSD Claim

### Stressor Documentation
The VA requires evidence of an in-service stressor event. For **combat veterans**, a stressor can be conceded based on service records showing deployment to a combat zone. For **MST claims**, the VA accepts a broader range of evidence including buddy statements, behavioral changes documented in service records, and counseling records.

### Nexus Letter
A strong nexus letter from a qualified psychologist or psychiatrist should explicitly state that your current PTSD diagnosis is **"at least as likely as not"** caused by or aggravated by your military service. The letter should reference your specific stressor events and explain the clinical connection.

### Buddy Statements
Statements from fellow service members who witnessed the stressor event or from family members who can describe behavioral changes after service are powerful supporting evidence.

## C&P Exam Tips for PTSD

Your Compensation and Pension exam is critical. Here is what to expect:

- **Be honest and specific** about your worst days, not your best. Many veterans downplay symptoms out of stoicism or habit.
- **Describe frequency and severity** of symptoms: How often do you have nightmares? How many times per week do you experience flashbacks? How does hypervigilance affect your daily routine?
- **Document social impairment** including difficulty maintaining relationships, social isolation, irritability with family members, and avoidance of crowds or public places.
- **Mention occupational impact** such as difficulty concentrating, conflicts with coworkers, inability to hold employment, or needing accommodations at work.
- **Bring a symptom log** if possible. Tracking your symptoms in the weeks before the exam gives the examiner concrete data points.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Filing without treatment records** — Even a few sessions with a VA mental health provider or private therapist strengthen your claim significantly.
2. **Vague stressor statements** — Be specific about dates, locations, units, and what happened. The more detail, the easier it is for the VA to verify.
3. **Not claiming secondary conditions** — PTSD frequently causes or worsens sleep apnea, migraines, hypertension, and substance use disorders. File secondary claims for these.
4. **Accepting a low initial rating without appeal** — If your rating does not reflect your actual level of impairment, file a Supplemental Claim or Higher-Level Review within one year.

## Related Conditions to Consider

Veterans with PTSD often qualify for additional ratings for [sleep apnea](/conditions/sleep-apnea), [migraines](/conditions/migraines), [tinnitus](/conditions/tinnitus), and [major depressive disorder](/conditions/depression). These secondary claims can significantly increase your combined rating.
