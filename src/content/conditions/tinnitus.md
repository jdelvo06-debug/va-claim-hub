# Tinnitus VA Disability Rating Guide

Tinnitus — the perception of ringing, buzzing, hissing, or other sounds in the ears without an external source — is the single most commonly claimed VA disability condition. In fiscal year 2023, over 2.7 million veterans received compensation for tinnitus.

## How the VA Rates Tinnitus

Tinnitus is rated under Diagnostic Code 6260. The **maximum schedular rating is 10%**, regardless of whether tinnitus is present in one or both ears. This was confirmed by the Federal Circuit in *Smith v. Nicholson* (2005).

### What 10% Means

A 10% rating for tinnitus currently pays approximately $171.23 per month (2024 rates). While this may seem modest on its own, tinnitus is valuable as part of a combined rating. It can push a veteran from 80% to 90% combined, which represents a significant monthly increase.

## Proving Your Tinnitus Claim

### Service Connection Evidence

The strongest tinnitus claims include:

- **Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) with noise exposure** — Infantry, artillery, aviation, armor, and other high-noise MOSs establish a presumption of hazardous noise exposure. The VA uses a Duty MOS Noise Exposure Listing to evaluate this.
- **Service treatment records** showing complaints of ear ringing or noise exposure.
- **Audiogram results** from service showing a shift in hearing thresholds, even if hearing loss does not meet the VA threshold for a separate rating.
- **A credible lay statement** describing when tinnitus began and how it has continued since service. Veterans are considered competent to report symptoms they can directly observe, and tinnitus is inherently subjective.

### Nexus Letter

While not always required for straightforward tinnitus claims (especially with a high-noise MOS), a nexus letter can strengthen borderline cases. The letter should explain how your noise exposure history is consistent with the onset of tinnitus and reference current medical literature on noise-induced cochlear damage.

## C&P Exam Tips

Tinnitus exams are typically brief and combined with a hearing loss evaluation. Key points:

- **Be specific about onset**: "The ringing started during my deployment to Kandahar in 2008 and has been constant since" is far more persuasive than "I have ringing in my ears."
- **Describe the character**: Is it high-pitched ringing, low buzzing, or pulsating? Is it constant or intermittent? Which ear, or both?
- **Explain the impact**: Does tinnitus interfere with sleep, concentration, conversations, or your ability to work in quiet environments?
- **Do not exaggerate hearing loss**: The audiometric testing is objective. Focus on accurately describing your tinnitus symptoms.

## Filing Strategy

Tinnitus is often filed alongside [hearing loss](/conditions/hearing-loss) as these conditions frequently co-occur. If you also experience balance issues, consider a claim for [Meniere's disease](/conditions/menieres-disease) or vestibular dysfunction.

Veterans with tinnitus caused by traumatic brain injury should ensure the TBI is separately rated, as the tinnitus may be service-connected as a secondary condition.
