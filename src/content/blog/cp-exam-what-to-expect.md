# What to Expect at Your VA C&P Exam

The Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam is arguably the most critical step in the VA disability claims process. This single appointment can determine your rating, your monthly compensation, and your eligibility for additional benefits. Despite its importance, many veterans walk in unprepared.

## What Is the C&P Exam

A C&P exam is a medical examination ordered by the VA to evaluate the **current severity** of your claimed condition and determine whether it is **connected to your military service**. The examiner — a VA physician, nurse practitioner, or contracted examiner — produces a Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ) that the VA rating official uses to assign your disability percentage.

The exam is **not a treatment appointment**. The examiner is there to document and evaluate, not to treat or advocate for you. Understanding this distinction is crucial.

## Before the Exam

### Gather Your Documentation
- Copies of your service treatment records
- Current medical records and imaging
- Your [nexus letter](/blog/how-to-write-nexus-letter) if you have one
- A personal statement describing your condition and its impact
- Any buddy statements you have collected

### Prepare Your Narrative
Practice articulating:
- When your symptoms started and how they relate to service
- Your **worst days**, not your best — this is not the time for military stoicism
- Specific examples of how the condition affects work, relationships, and daily activities
- Frequency and duration of symptoms (attacks per month, hours of sleep lost, etc.)

### Day-of Preparation
- Arrive 15 minutes early with a government-issued ID
- Do not take extra medication to mask symptoms before the exam
- Do not perform stretches or warm-ups before a musculoskeletal exam — they want to see your natural baseline
- Bring your headache diary, pain log, or symptom tracker if you keep one

## During the Exam

### For Mental Health Conditions
The examiner will conduct a clinical interview lasting 30 to 90 minutes. They will ask about:
- Current symptoms and their severity
- Relationship and social functioning
- Occupational functioning and work history
- Suicidal ideation (be honest — this is a clinical assessment, not a gatekeeping question)
- Substance use history
- Treatment history and medication response

### For Musculoskeletal Conditions
The examiner will:
- Measure range of motion using a goniometer
- Test joint stability
- Assess functional loss during flare-ups
- Document pain on motion (the degree where pain begins)
- Per *Correia v. McDonald*, they must test active, passive, weight-bearing, and non-weight-bearing range of motion

### For All Conditions
- Answer every question thoroughly. If a question is ambiguous, ask for clarification.
- If the examiner does not ask about a relevant symptom, volunteer the information.
- Do not minimize your condition. Military culture trains us to push through pain and underreport — this works against you in a C&P exam.

## After the Exam

The examiner will submit their report to the VA, typically within a few days. You can request a copy of the DBQ through your VA representative or by accessing your claim file.

### Red Flags in the Examiner's Report
If the examiner's findings do not match your experience, you can:
- Request a review by your VSO
- Submit a rebuttal with your own medical evidence
- Request a new exam with a different examiner
- File a Higher-Level Review or Supplemental Claim if the final rating is based on an inadequate exam

## The Biggest Mistake Veterans Make

Treating the C&P exam like a military fitness evaluation. The instinct to say "I'm fine" or to demonstrate maximum effort during range of motion testing works directly against you. The examiner can only rate what they observe and what you report. If you minimize your symptoms, your rating will reflect that minimized version of your condition.

Be honest, be thorough, and describe your condition on your worst days — not the day you happen to feel okay.
