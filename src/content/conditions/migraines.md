# Migraines VA Disability Rating Guide

Migraine headaches are a common condition among veterans, frequently connected to traumatic brain injuries, PTSD, environmental exposures, and neck injuries sustained during military service. The VA rates migraines based on frequency and severity of attacks.

## How the VA Rates Migraines

Migraines are rated under Diagnostic Code 8100 based on the frequency of **prostrating** attacks — episodes severe enough to force you to stop what you are doing and rest:

**0% — Less frequent attacks**
Diagnosed migraines that occur infrequently or are well-controlled with medication.

**10% — Characteristic prostrating attacks averaging one in 2 months over the last several months**
Roughly six prostrating attacks per year.

**30% — Characteristic prostrating attacks occurring on average once a month over the last several months**
Approximately twelve prostrating episodes per year, representing a significant burden on daily functioning.

**50% — Very frequent completely prostrating and prolonged attacks productive of severe economic inadaptability**
This is the maximum rating and requires demonstrating that your migraines are so frequent and severe that they substantially interfere with your ability to work and earn a living. This does not require total unemployability — "severe economic inadaptability" has been interpreted to mean significant interference with gainful employment.

## What "Prostrating" Means

The VA defines prostrating attacks as headaches that are **severe enough to cause you to cease normal activity and require rest** in a quiet, dark room. Key indicators include:

- Inability to continue working or performing daily tasks
- Need to lie down in a dark, quiet environment
- Duration of several hours to multiple days
- Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, photophobia (light sensitivity), phonophobia (sound sensitivity), visual aura

## Building Your Claim

### Headache Diary
A contemporaneous headache log is one of the most powerful pieces of evidence for a migraine claim. Track:
- Date and time of onset
- Duration of each episode
- Severity on a 1-10 scale
- Whether the attack was prostrating (did you have to stop activity and rest?)
- Medications taken and their effectiveness
- Triggers identified
- Impact on work, social activities, and daily functioning

### Medical Evidence
- Treatment records showing consistent migraine treatment
- Imaging (MRI/CT) if performed to rule out other causes
- Prescription records for migraine medications (triptans, preventatives like topiramate or propranolol)

### Nexus Letter
If claiming secondary to TBI, PTSD, or a cervical spine condition, a nexus letter explaining the medical mechanism is essential. Research supports established connections between these conditions and migraine development.

## C&P Exam Preparation

- **Bring your headache diary** to the exam.
- **Be precise about frequency**: "I average 3-4 prostrating migraines per month, each lasting 8 to 24 hours" is more compelling than "I get headaches a lot."
- **Explain economic impact**: Missed work days, reduced hours, inability to maintain consistent employment, or needing accommodations.
- **Describe the full picture**: Aura symptoms, prodromal signs, postdromal fatigue, and the total time each migraine affects your function (not just the peak headache phase).

## Secondary Connections

Migraines are commonly rated secondary to:
- **Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)** — Post-traumatic headache is one of the most common TBI sequelae
- **PTSD** — The stress-headache connection is well-documented in medical literature
- **Cervical spine conditions** — Cervicogenic headaches arising from neck pathology
- **Tinnitus** — Some veterans experience migraines triggered by severe tinnitus

Consider filing for these primary conditions if not already service-connected, then linking migraines as secondary.
