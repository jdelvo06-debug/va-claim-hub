# Sleep Apnea VA Disability Rating Guide

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is one of the most valuable VA disability ratings a veteran can receive, with the majority of veterans rated at 50% for CPAP use. It is also one of the most commonly claimed as a secondary condition to PTSD, obesity, and other service-connected disabilities.

## How the VA Rates Sleep Apnea

Sleep apnea is rated under Diagnostic Code 6847 with four possible ratings:

**0% — Asymptomatic but documented sleep disorder**
A sleep study confirms the diagnosis, but the condition does not require treatment or produce symptoms that affect daily functioning.

**30% — Persistent daytime hypersomnolence**
You experience excessive daytime sleepiness that is documented and affects your daily functioning, but you do not require a breathing assistance device.

**50% — Requires use of a breathing assistance device (CPAP)**
This is the most common rating. If your sleep study confirms a diagnosis and your treating physician prescribes a CPAP, BiPAP, or similar device, you qualify for 50%. This rating pays approximately $1,041.82 per month (2024 single veteran rate).

**100% — Chronic respiratory failure with carbon dioxide retention or cor pulmonale, or requires tracheostomy**
Reserved for the most severe cases involving significant cardiopulmonary complications.

## Building a Strong Sleep Apnea Claim

### Direct Service Connection

Direct service connection requires evidence that sleep apnea began during or was caused by military service. Supporting evidence includes:

- In-service complaints of snoring, choking during sleep, or excessive fatigue
- Buddy statements from bunkmates describing loud snoring or observed apneic episodes
- Service treatment records documenting sleep complaints or fatigue
- A sleep study confirming the diagnosis (required for all ratings)

### Secondary Service Connection

Sleep apnea is frequently claimed secondary to:

- **PTSD** — Research has established a bidirectional relationship between PTSD and OSA. The hyperarousal state associated with PTSD can worsen sleep apnea, and sleep fragmentation from apnea can exacerbate PTSD symptoms.
- **Weight gain from service-connected conditions** — If a service-connected knee injury, back condition, or medication side effects caused weight gain, and that weight gain caused or worsened sleep apnea, the connection is viable.
- **Sinusitis or rhinitis** — Chronic nasal inflammation can contribute to upper airway obstruction during sleep.

### The Nexus Letter Is Critical

For secondary claims especially, a well-written nexus letter from a physician who understands VA disability criteria is essential. The letter must explain the medical mechanism connecting your service-connected condition to your sleep apnea diagnosis, citing relevant medical literature.

## C&P Exam Preparation

- **Bring your sleep study results** to the exam. If your sleep study is older than a few years, consider getting an updated one.
- **Document your CPAP compliance** — The VA may check if you are actually using your prescribed device.
- **Describe your symptoms thoroughly**: How many times do you wake up at night? Do you experience morning headaches? How does daytime fatigue affect your ability to work and function?
- **If claiming secondary**, be prepared to explain how your primary condition led to or worsened your sleep apnea.

## Important Regulatory Note

The VA proposed rule changes in 2024 that could affect how sleep apnea is rated in the future, potentially requiring a minimum AHI (Apnea-Hypopnea Index) score for each rating level. Veterans with pending claims should be aware of potential changes to the rating schedule. File your claim promptly to preserve your rights under the current criteria.

## Related Claims

Veterans with sleep apnea should also evaluate claims for [PTSD](/conditions/ptsd) (if not already rated), [hypertension](/conditions/hypertension) secondary to sleep apnea, and [GERD](/conditions/gerd) which frequently co-occurs with OSA.
