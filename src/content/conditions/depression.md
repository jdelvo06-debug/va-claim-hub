# Depression VA Disability Rating Guide

Major depressive disorder (MDD) affects a significant number of veterans, whether stemming directly from military experiences or developing secondary to chronic pain, traumatic brain injury, or other service-connected conditions. Depression is rated under the same General Rating Formula for Mental Disorders as PTSD.

## Rating Criteria

Depression uses the same 0% to 100% scale as all mental health conditions under 38 CFR 4.130. The key factor is the degree of **occupational and social impairment**.

**Important note**: The VA will not assign separate ratings for PTSD and depression. If you have both conditions, the VA rates them together under a single mental health rating. However, a veteran rated for PTSD at 50% may be able to increase to 70% if depression symptoms compound their overall impairment.

## Direct vs. Secondary Connection

### Direct Service Connection
Evidence that depression began during or was caused by military service:
- In-service mental health treatment records
- Documentation of stressful military events
- Buddy statements describing mood changes during service
- Evidence of prescription antidepressants during service

### Secondary Service Connection
Depression is frequently claimed secondary to:
- **Chronic pain conditions** (back, knee, shoulder) — chronic pain is one of the strongest predictors of depression
- **Traumatic Brain Injury** — depression is a recognized TBI comorbidity
- **Loss of physical capability** from service-connected injuries
- **Tinnitus** — persistent ringing has been clinically associated with depression and anxiety

A nexus letter from a psychiatrist or psychologist explaining how your service-connected condition caused or worsened your depression is essential for secondary claims.

## C&P Exam Tips

The mental health C&P exam involves a clinical interview lasting 30 to 90 minutes. Prepare by:

- Being specific about how depression affects your daily routine, relationships, and work capacity
- Describing your worst periods, not just current functioning
- Mentioning any suicidal ideation (past or present) — this is a significant indicator the VA considers
- Discussing sleep disturbance, appetite changes, loss of interest in activities, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, and feelings of worthlessness
- Bringing a list of medications and treatment history

## Common Mistakes

1. Not seeking treatment before filing — even a few documented visits strengthen your claim
2. Minimizing symptoms during the C&P exam out of pride or habit
3. Not explaining how depression compounds other service-connected conditions
4. Failing to document the impact on work attendance, relationships, and daily functioning
