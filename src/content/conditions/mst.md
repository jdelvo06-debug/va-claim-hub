# Military Sexual Trauma (MST) VA Disability Rating Guide

Military Sexual Trauma (MST) refers to sexual assault or repeated, threatening sexual harassment that occurred during military service. MST is not itself a ratable condition — rather, it functions as a **stressor** that can cause or contribute to ratable mental health conditions, most commonly PTSD, depression, and anxiety disorders.

The VA has specific rules designed to make it easier for MST survivors to receive benefits without the same documentation burden required for combat-related claims.

## What the VA Rates

The VA does not assign a rating for "MST" as a diagnosis. Instead, it rates the **mental health condition caused by MST**, typically:

- **PTSD** (most common) — rated under 38 CFR 4.130, DC 9411
- **Major Depressive Disorder** — rated under DC 9434
- **Generalized Anxiety Disorder** — rated under DC 9400
- **Other Specified Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorder** (OSTRA) — rated under DC 9440

Rating levels follow the **General Rating Formula for Mental Disorders**: 0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%, based on the degree of occupational and social impairment.

## The Relaxed Evidentiary Standard for MST

For combat claims, the VA can concede a stressor based on service records showing combat deployment. MST claims are different — perpetrators are often not reported, and official records frequently do not document the assault. Congress and the VA have addressed this with a **significantly relaxed evidentiary standard** for MST stressors.

### Alternative Evidence the VA Accepts

When official records do not document the MST event, the VA can accept:

- **Behavioral markers in service records**: unexplained requests for unit or duty station transfer, sudden decline in work performance, disciplinary action following the incident
- **Medical records**: documentation of anxiety, depression, or other mental health conditions near the time of the assault
- **Counseling records**: notes from military chaplains, mental health providers, or civilian counselors — even if the assault itself is not explicitly described
- **Statements from friends, family, or colleagues**: describing behavioral changes they observed
- **Law enforcement or civilian records**: police reports, restraining orders, or court records from civilian incidents that overlap with the service period
- **Personal journals or written statements**: personal accounts written near the time of the incident

This means **a veteran does not need to have reported the MST at the time of service** to establish the stressor. The threshold is credibility, not documentation.

## The VA's MST Coordinator Program

Every VA medical center has a designated **MST Coordinator** — a clinical staff member who can help survivors access VA healthcare, mental health services, and benefits support at no cost. VA MST-related healthcare is free of charge to veterans, **regardless of service-connected status**, making it one of the few VA benefits available without a disability rating.

Contact your local VA Medical Center and ask for the MST Coordinator before or during the claims process.

## Building a Strong MST Claim

### Step 1: Get VA Mental Health Treatment
A diagnosis from a VA mental health provider is the most powerful piece of evidence in your claim. VA treatment records documenting PTSD or another mental health condition linked to an MST experience carry significant weight.

### Step 2: Work with a VSO or MST Specialist
Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs) experienced in MST claims can help you identify and gather alternative evidence and draft a compelling personal statement. The American Legion, DAV, and VFW all have VSO representatives experienced with MST claims.

### Step 3: Write a Detailed Personal Statement
A personal statement (VA Form 21-4138 or a separate written account) describing what happened, the circumstances, and how the experience has affected your mental health and daily life is one of the most important documents in your claim. You do not need to use explicit language — describe the impact and your experience in your own words.

### Step 4: Obtain a Nexus Letter
A nexus letter from a qualified psychologist or psychiatrist explicitly connecting your current diagnosis to your in-service MST experience is essential, particularly if VA treatment records are limited.

## C&P Exam Tips for MST Claims

C&P exams for MST-related conditions are governed by special VA guidelines:

- **You can request a same-gender examiner** — and the VA is required to make every reasonable effort to accommodate that request. Make this request in writing well before your exam date.
- **You may bring a support person** — a trusted friend, family member, or advocate can accompany you.
- **You do not have to describe the assault in detail** — focus on describing your current symptoms and how they affect your daily functioning, work, and relationships.
- **Be honest about your worst days** — the examiner is rating your impairment, not your resilience.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not reporting MST to the VA out of shame or fear** — The VA is legally required to maintain confidentiality around MST disclosures in most circumstances.
2. **Assuming lack of documentation means no claim** — The relaxed evidentiary standard exists precisely because MST is chronically underreported.
3. **Not claiming secondary conditions** — Conditions caused by medications (weight gain, metabolic issues) or physical injuries associated with the MST event may be independently ratable.
4. **Skipping VA mental health treatment** — Free VA mental health care for MST is available regardless of claim status; using it also builds the clinical record that supports your claim.
