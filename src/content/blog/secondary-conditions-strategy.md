# Secondary Service Connection: How to Increase Your VA Rating

If you are already service-connected for one or more conditions, you may be leaving significant compensation on the table by not filing for secondary conditions. A **secondary service connection** claim establishes that a new disability was **caused by or aggravated by** an already service-connected condition.

## How Secondary Claims Work

Under 38 CFR 3.310, the VA must grant service connection for a disability that is "proximately due to or the result of a service-connected disease or injury." This includes conditions that are **directly caused** by a primary condition and conditions that are **aggravated** (permanently worsened) by a primary condition.

### Direct Causation vs. Aggravation

**Direct causation**: Your service-connected knee injury altered your gait, which caused a hip condition that would not have developed otherwise.

**Aggravation**: You had a pre-existing mild blood pressure condition, but your service-connected sleep apnea permanently worsened it beyond its natural progression. The VA rates the degree of aggravation — the difference between the baseline severity and the current severity.

## Common Secondary Claim Combinations

### PTSD Secondary Conditions
- Sleep apnea (bidirectional relationship with PTSD)
- Migraines (stress-mediated headache pathway)
- Hypertension (chronic cortisol elevation)
- GERD (gut-brain axis dysfunction)
- Bruxism/TMJ (clenching from hyperarousal)
- Erectile dysfunction (PTSD medications, psychological factors)
- Substance use disorder (self-medication)

### Musculoskeletal Secondary Conditions
- Opposite joint strain (compensating for a bad knee leads to contralateral knee problems)
- Back conditions from altered gait
- Radiculopathy from spinal conditions
- Depression from chronic pain and limited mobility
- Weight gain leading to sleep apnea

### Sleep Apnea Secondary Conditions
- Hypertension (nocturnal oxygen desaturation)
- Heart disease (cardiac remodeling from recurrent hypoxia)
- Depression (sleep fragmentation and fatigue)
- GERD (increased intra-abdominal pressure)

## Filing Strategy

### Step 1: Identify Potential Secondary Conditions
Review your currently service-connected conditions and honestly assess what other health problems they have caused or worsened. Consult your primary care provider about connections between your conditions.

### Step 2: Get Diagnosed
Before filing, ensure you have a formal diagnosis of the secondary condition from a qualified medical provider. The VA cannot rate a condition without a current diagnosis.

### Step 3: Obtain a Nexus Letter
A nexus letter from a medical provider explaining the causal or aggravation relationship between your primary and secondary conditions is critical. The letter should cite medical literature supporting the connection and use the "at least as likely as not" standard.

### Step 4: File the Claim
Submit VA Form 21-526EZ indicating the secondary condition and specifying which service-connected condition caused or aggravated it. Include your nexus letter, diagnosis documentation, and any supporting evidence.

## VA Math: How Combined Ratings Work

VA disability ratings are not simply added together. They use a **combined ratings table** where each additional rating is applied to the remaining (non-disabled) percentage. For example:

- 50% + 30% does not equal 80%
- Instead: 50% leaves 50% remaining. 30% of 50% = 15%. Total = 65%, rounded to 70%.

This means every additional secondary rating adds meaningful monthly compensation, especially at higher combined levels where the marginal value of each percentage point increases.

## The Bottom Line

Most veterans are underrated because they have not filed for conditions secondary to their primary disabilities. If your service-connected knee causes back problems, if your PTSD worsens your blood pressure, or if your pain medications damage your stomach lining — those are compensable secondary conditions.
