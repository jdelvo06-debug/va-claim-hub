# How to Appeal a VA Disability Denial

A VA denial is not the end of the road — it is the beginning of the appeals process. The vast majority of veterans who appeal a denial eventually receive benefits. Understanding your three appeal options and choosing the right one for your situation is critical to getting the rating you deserve.

## Why Claims Get Denied

Before choosing an appeal lane, understand *why* your claim was denied. The denial letter will cite a specific reason. Common reasons include:

- **No nexus established** — The VA could not connect your condition to your military service
- **Insufficient medical evidence** — No current diagnosis or inadequate medical records
- **Condition not service-connected** — The VA determined the condition predated or is unrelated to service
- **No current disability** — A diagnosis exists but the VA found no ratable functional impairment
- **DBQ inadequate** — The C&P exam report was incomplete or the examiner's opinion was negative

Identifying the specific reason determines which appeal lane gives you the best chance of success.

## The Three Appeal Lanes (AMA Framework)

The Appeals Modernization Act (AMA), effective February 2019, created three separate lanes for appealing VA decisions. You have one year from the date of your rating decision to choose a lane.

### Lane 1: Supplemental Claim

**Best for:** Claims denied due to lack of evidence, inadequate nexus, or missing medical records.

A Supplemental Claim allows you to submit **new and relevant evidence** that was not previously considered. The VA is required to re-adjudicate your claim using this new evidence.

**What counts as "new and relevant" evidence:**
- A nexus letter from a private physician (the most powerful addition)
- Private medical records documenting your diagnosis and severity
- Buddy statements from family or fellow veterans
- A completed DBQ from your treating physician
- Medical literature supporting the service connection

**Key advantages:**
- Fastest lane to a decision
- If approved, your effective date goes back to the original claim date
- You can loop back to a Supplemental Claim after any other appeal if you obtain new evidence

**Strategy tip:** If your claim was denied for lack of nexus, get a private nexus letter before filing. Do not file a Supplemental Claim without new, substantive evidence — you will likely receive the same denial.

### Lane 2: Higher-Level Review (HLR)

**Best for:** Claims denied due to a rating error, missed evidence, or a flawed C&P exam that is already part of the record.

A Higher-Level Review assigns a senior VA claims adjudicator to review your existing record. **You cannot submit new evidence** in this lane. The reviewer looks for clear error in how the original decision was made.

**When HLR is the right choice:**
- The C&P examiner made a factual error (e.g., incorrectly described your service history)
- The VA ignored evidence already in your file (e.g., service treatment records that clearly document the condition)
- The wrong diagnostic code was applied, resulting in an incorrect rating percentage
- The decision contains a legal error (e.g., failure to apply benefit of the doubt)

**The informal conference option:** You can request an informal conference call with the reviewer to point out specific errors. Take this option — it is your chance to guide the reviewer to the problems in the decision.

**Strategy tip:** Review your claims file (C-file) before filing an HLR. Request it through the VA's eBenefits portal or at your regional office. You need to know exactly what is in the record to argue that the reviewer missed something.

### Lane 3: Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA)

**Best for:** Complex cases, cases requiring a hearing, cases where you want a Veterans Law Judge to review the claim, or after failing both other lanes.

The Board of Veterans' Appeals is an independent body that reviews VA decisions. There are three docket options within the BVA lane:

**Direct Review** — No hearing, no new evidence. The judge reviews the existing record. Fastest BVA option (estimated 1–2 years).

**Evidence Submission** — No hearing, but you can submit new evidence within 90 days of filing the BVA appeal. Useful when you need more time to gather evidence but do not need a hearing.

**Hearing Request** — A in-person or virtual hearing before a Veterans Law Judge, followed by 90 days to submit new evidence. Longest wait (3–5+ years) but most thorough review.

**Key BVA advantages:**
- Veterans Law Judges are more independent than regional office adjudicators
- BVA decisions can be appealed to the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims (CAVC) if denied

**Strategy tip:** Consider obtaining a Nexus Opinion from a veteran-friendly private physician and filing via the Evidence Submission docket. The combination of a skilled judge and strong new evidence wins many cases that regional offices denied.

## Choosing the Right Lane

| Situation | Recommended Lane |
|---|---|
| Denied for lack of nexus — have not yet obtained a nexus letter | Supplemental Claim (after getting nexus letter) |
| Denied but strong evidence already in file — possible adjudicator error | Higher-Level Review |
| Previously denied under the old appeals system before 2019 | Contact a VSO — may need specific transition filing |
| Complex multi-condition case or want a hearing | BVA – Hearing Request |
| Want the fastest possible resolution | Supplemental Claim or HLR |

## Working With a VSO or VA Attorney

You do not need to navigate the appeals process alone. Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs) such as the DAV, VFW, American Legion, and others provide free claims assistance. An **accredited VA attorney** (who works on contingency — paid only if you win) can be invaluable for complex BVA cases, especially on appeal to the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims.

## The Bottom Line

Do not accept a denial as final. The evidence standard in VA claims — "at least as likely as not" (50% + 1) — is one of the lowest in law. With the right evidence and the right appeal lane, most denials can be overturned.
