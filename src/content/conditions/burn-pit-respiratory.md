# Burn Pit & Toxic Exposure (PACT Act) VA Disability Rating Guide

The **Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics (PACT) Act of 2022** is the most significant expansion of VA benefits in decades. It establishes presumptive service connection for dozens of conditions affecting post-9/11 veterans exposed to burn pits, airborne hazards, radiation, and other toxic substances — eliminating the need to prove a direct link between exposure and illness for covered conditions.

## What Are Burn Pits?

Burn pits were open-air waste disposal sites used extensively at U.S. military installations in Iraq, Afghanistan, Djibouti, and other locations. Everything from human waste and medical waste to plastics, chemicals, ammunition, and aircraft parts was burned. Veterans stationed near these pits were exposed to toxic smoke containing dioxins, heavy metals, volatile organic compounds, and other hazardous materials for months or years.

The Airborne Hazards and Open Burn Pit Registry (AHOBPR) was established to track this exposure, and veterans who completed the registry questionnaire have additional documentation of their exposure history.

## Who Qualifies Under the PACT Act

The PACT Act covers veterans who:

- Served in a **Post-9/11 combat zone** (Iraq, Afghanistan, Syria, Djibouti, or other GWOT locations) on or after August 2, 1990
- Were **stationed near a burn pit or had airborne hazard exposure** during service
- **Vietnam-era veterans** (certain additional Agent Orange locations and Blue Water Navy)
- **Radiation-exposed veterans** (nuclear testing, occupation of Hiroshima/Nagasaki, etc.)
- **Camp Lejeune veterans** (contaminated water exposure between 1953–1987)

## PACT Act Presumptive Conditions

### Respiratory Conditions (Effective August 10, 2022)

The following respiratory conditions are now presumptively service-connected for eligible veterans:

- **Constrictive bronchiolitis** and **obliterative bronchiolitis**
- **Constrictive pericarditis**
- **Granulomatous disease**
- **Sarcoidosis**
- **Rhinitis** (allergic, non-allergic, atrophic, and reactive)
- **Rhinosinusitis** and **sinusitis** (all types)
- **Laryngitis** and **laryngotracheitis**
- **Pharyngitis** and **tonsillitis**
- **Nasopharyngitis**

### Cancers (Effective August 10, 2022)

Head/neck cancers, respiratory cancers, and urinary tract cancers are broadly covered. Specifically:

- Any cancer of the head, neck, respiratory system, or reproductive system
- **Melanoma**
- **Squamous cell carcinoma** of the head, neck, or throat
- **Glioblastoma** and other brain cancers
- **Pancreatic cancer**
- **Kidney cancer** and **bladder cancer**
- **Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma** and other lymphatic cancers
- **Any malignancy** for which a positive association with burn pit/toxic exposure has been recognized

> **Note**: Cancer ratings generally start at **100%** during active treatment and are then reevaluated at post-treatment follow-ups for any continuing disability.

## Respiratory Conditions: Rating Overview

Chronic respiratory conditions from burn pit exposure are typically rated under one of these diagnostic codes:

**Rhinitis/Sinusitis (DC 6501–6513)**
- Rhinitis and sinusitis are rated 0–50% based on severity, frequency of incapacitating episodes, and need for surgery
- Many veterans qualify for 10%–30% for persistent sinusitis or rhinitis requiring ongoing treatment

**Laryngitis/Pharyngitis/Nasopharyngitis (DC 6516–6520)**
- Rated 0%–30% based on severity and chronicity

**Bronchiolitis/Bronchitis (DC 6600)**
- Chronic bronchitis rated 10%–30% based on FEV1 results and severity of symptoms

**Asthma (DC 6602)**
- Frequently claimed by veterans with airborne hazard exposure
- Rated 10%–100% based on peak flow rates, FEV1, and frequency of attacks

**Obstructive/Restrictive Pulmonary Disease (DC 6604)**
- Rated based on pulmonary function testing (FVC, FEV1/FVC ratio)

## How to File Under the PACT Act

### Step 1: Register with the Burn Pit Registry
If you haven't already, complete the **Airborne Hazards and Open Burn Pit Registry** questionnaire at VA.gov. This does not replace a formal claim but creates a documented exposure record.

### Step 2: Gather Service Records Confirming Exposure Location
- DD-214 showing deployment to a covered location
- Unit deployment orders showing assignment to specific bases where burn pits were documented
- Any letters, emails, or other documentation referencing burn pit proximity during service

### Step 3: Obtain Medical Evidence of Your Current Condition
- Pulmonary function testing (spirometry) from a pulmonologist for respiratory claims
- Biopsy, imaging, or oncology records for cancer claims
- Primary care or ENT records for rhinitis/sinusitis claims

### Step 4: File VA Form 21-526EZ
Under the PACT Act, list your conditions and cite presumptive service connection under 38 CFR 3.309(f) or the PACT Act provisions. A VSO can help ensure you're citing the correct regulatory basis.

## C&P Exam Tips

- **Bring pulmonary function test results** — spirometry is essential for most respiratory ratings
- **Describe symptom frequency and severity**: How often do you have sinus infections? How many antibiotic courses per year? Do you use a rescue inhaler? How often?
- **For cancer claims**: Bring all oncology records including diagnosis date, treatment history, and current surveillance scans
- **Don't minimize symptoms** — many veterans describe symptoms as "not that bad" during exams when their daily functioning tells a different story

## Secondary Conditions

Veterans with burn pit-related respiratory conditions should also file for:

- **[Depression](/conditions/depression)** — chronic respiratory illness has well-documented associations with depression and anxiety
- **Sleep disturbances and [sleep apnea](/conditions/sleep-apnea)** — obstructive breathing from sinusitis and respiratory conditions
- **Hypertension** — chronic respiratory conditions can cause or worsen cardiovascular conditions

## PACT Act Retroactive Benefits

Veterans who were previously denied benefits for conditions now covered by the PACT Act can **reapply without facing the standard bar on reopening denied claims**. The VA is required to proactively notify eligible veterans and reprocess previously denied claims. If you were denied for a condition that is now a PACT Act presumptive, file a Supplemental Claim with evidence of your service dates.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not registering with the Burn Pit Registry** before filing — while not required, it strengthens your documentation
2. **Filing only for one condition** — many veterans have multiple PACT Act-covered conditions; file comprehensive claims
3. **Not claiming cancer at 100% during active treatment** — all active cancers start at 100%; don't undervalue this
4. **Waiting too long after diagnosis** — the PACT Act eliminates many barriers, but the sooner you file, the earlier your effective date
5. **Not pursuing secondary conditions** — PACT Act conditions often cause depression, sleep disorders, and cardiovascular issues that are separately ratable
